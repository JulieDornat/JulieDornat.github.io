F1 Team Recognition with CNN

Date: December 2024

Context: APP5 Project - Polytech Paris-Saclay

Team: Collaboration with Maeva Burillo

Technologies: Python, TensorFlow

Tools: Google Colab, GitHub

Libraries / Modules: Pillow, Keras, NumPy

Context

Artificial intelligence is transforming many industries, including motorsport. Formula 1 is a highly technical sport that brings together engineers and scientists to design high-performance race cars capable of completing circuits in extremely short times.

Today, AI plays an important role in this domain. It is used to model aerodynamic interactions between the car and air flows, simulate race strategies while taking environmental conditions into account, and anticipate mechanical failures.

Beyond being a competitive sport, Formula 1 constantly pushes technological boundaries. Each season, new car models are developed and optimized in order to improve performance and competitiveness.

Problem

The goal of this project was to develop an artificial intelligence model capable of recognizing Formula 1 cars from images. The first objective was to automatically identify the team associated with a car present in a photograph. In a second step, the objective was to predict the exact model of the car in order to estimate the season in which the image was taken (between 2019 and 2024).

This project was carried out in collaboration with Maeva Burillo.

Method

Throughout the project, we followed a standard machine learning workflow inspired by the methodology presented in the book Hands-On Machine Learning with Scikit-Learn and TensorFlow by Aurélien Géron, as well as the structure introduced during the course. The main steps included problem framing, data collection, data preparation, model design, training, validation, performance analysis, and optimization.

Since we were not yet familiar with Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA), this step was not fully performed in the project.

During the development process, we organized regular meetings to make key decisions and distribute tasks. This division of work allowed each team member to progress independently while maintaining coordination on the overall project.

Problem Framing

Recognizing Formula 1 teams and car models from images is a multi-class classification problem. Currently, ten Formula 1 teams compete in the championship, and each team designs new car models every season. Visual differences between models can be subtle, making the classification task particularly challenging.

Dataset

The next step consisted of building datasets suitable for training and evaluating the models. Publicly available datasets were either limited or contained corrupted images. Therefore, we developed a Python script to automatically collect images from the internet using keyword-based searches.

Data Preprocessing

The dataset was divided into three subsets: 80% for training, 10% for validation, and 10% for testing. The dataset was relatively small. For the team recognition task, the dataset contained 1,258 training images, 157 validation images, and 157 test images. For the F1 model recognition task, the dataset contained 1,627 training images, 185 validation images, and 243 test images.

Images were manually reviewed in order to remove corrupted or irrelevant files. They were then resized and normalized to make them compatible with deep learning models.

To mitigate overfitting observed during early experiments, data augmentation techniques were applied to the training set. Additional images were generated through transformations such as:

  • Rotations (up to 45 degrees)
  • Horizontal and vertical translations
  • Scaling transformations

Finally, each image was labeled according to its class. Classes were represented by numerical indices. For example, if an image corresponded to the Haas team, it was assigned the label 0.

Model

We designed an image classification model based on a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) implemented with TensorFlow. The architecture consists of several blocks of convolutional layers followed by max-pooling layers, which progressively extract visual features from the images.

Convolutional layers apply filters across the image in order to detect patterns such as shapes, edges, and textures. The number of filters and kernel sizes are tunable parameters that influence the richness of extracted features. Increasing the number of filters allows the model to capture more complex patterns but also increases computational cost and the risk of overfitting.

Max-pooling layers reduce the spatial dimensions of the feature maps by keeping only the maximum values within small regions. For example, using a 2×2 pooling window reduces the image dimensions by half. This operation decreases computational complexity while preserving the most important features.

These layers allow the model to progressively learn visual features, from simple patterns such as colors and shapes to more abstract elements specific to Formula 1 cars, such as chassis structures.

After feature extraction, a flatten layer converts the feature maps into a vector that can be processed by dense layers. These layers use the ReLU activation function to capture non-linear relationships.

The final dense layer uses a Softmax activation function to output a probability distribution over the classes. Each neuron corresponds to a specific team class.

During early experiments, we observed significant overfitting. To address this issue, dropout layers were introduced. These layers randomly deactivate a fraction of neurons during training, which helps prevent the model from relying too heavily on specific features and improves generalization.

Hyperparameter tuning was performed manually; no automated pipeline was implemented.

Model Training

During training, we defined both the number of epochs and the batch size. An epoch represents one complete pass through the training dataset, while a batch corresponds to a subset of images processed simultaneously before updating the model parameters.

We selected a batch size of 40, which was appropriate for the dataset size. A batch size that is too large may lead to suboptimal convergence, while a batch size that is too small can slow down training and produce unstable updates.

The training data were shuffled at each epoch to reduce bias related to the order of the samples and improve generalization.

For backpropagation, we used the SparseCategoricalCrossEntropy loss function, which is suitable for multi-class classification tasks. The optimizer used was Adam, which updates model parameters by computing gradients and adjusting weights accordingly.

The training process lasted approximately 2.5 hours.

Evaluation

After training, the model achieved an accuracy of approximately 76% for the Formula 1 team recognition task. Considering the relatively small size of the dataset, this result was considered satisfactory.

We analyzed the training results using accuracy and loss curves, a classification report, and a confusion matrix.

During overfitting, the model memorized the training data: the training loss approached zero while the validation loss increased. Training accuracy became very high, whereas validation accuracy remained below 50%.

After introducing regularization techniques and data augmentation, the training and validation curves became more consistent. Training was stopped at epoch 40 to prevent further overfitting.

The classification report allowed us to evaluate performance for each class, while the confusion matrix helped identify teams that were frequently confused. For example, the Williams class achieved the best performance, whereas Alpine was often confused with Ferrari or Red Bull Racing.

This may be explained by several factors. Background elements in images can influence predictions; for example, Alpine cars are often photographed on specific circuits, which may introduce biases. In addition, the Alpine logo is sometimes less visible in the images.

Potential improvements include increasing dataset diversity, refining the dataset cleaning process, and experimenting with different model architectures.

F1 Model Recognition

To recognize specific Formula 1 car models, we adopted a transfer learning approach based on the previously trained model. More precisely, we applied fine-tuning by modifying the final layer of the network in order to classify 48 different car models.

This task is significantly more challenging because differences between models within the same team are often subtle. Visual features such as color or logos are no longer sufficient to distinguish the cars.

The classification report revealed large variations between classes. Some models achieved accuracies above 70%, while others had very low performance. This difference is mainly explained by the limited amount of available data and the complexity of distinguishing visually similar models.
